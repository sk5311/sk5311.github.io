Exploring Tribal Broadband Access

This is an exploratory atlas documenting broadband infrastructure and Indigenous efforts toward digital equity in California.

Broadband Context in the United States

This map provides a comprehensive overview of broadband access across the United States, utilizing data distributed by the FCC. It emphasizes California as a particularly compelling case study due to its high number of BEAD-eligible locations, which include households categorized as underserved or unserved (i.e. possessing internet download/upload speeds of less than 100/20 Mbps and 25/3Mbps).

Middle Mile Infrastructure and Tribal Lands in California

The first map combines several data layers, including tribal government office locations, federally recognized tribal lands, and the existing middle-mile broadband network. It highlights which tribal governments reside on federally recognized lands—revealing that many do not—and evaluates the proximity of these communities to broadband infrastructure. The second highlights the challenges of extending last-mile broadband connections to remote tribal areas and provides a lens for understanding how physical distance from middle-mile infrastructure can exacerbate digital inequities.

Tribally Owned Broadband Infrastructure across the United States

Luckily, many tribes have opted to adopt their own broadband infrastructure to address these issues while also protecting Indigenous cultural values and tribal data. By establishing and managing their own networks, tribal governments can ensure equitable access to critical services like education, healthcare, and governance, while also safeguarding sensitive cultural information.

Indigenous Representation and Broadband Inequities

This section presents two maps highlighting broadband inequities and Indigenous representation across California. The first map visualizes BEAD-eligible locations alongside the percentage of Indigenous people in each census tract, revealing areas most in need of broadband investment. The second map, using the same percentage of Indigenous people in each census tract, evaluates the current status of tribally owned broadband networks. Together, these maps highlight potential opportunities for targeted investments to help support the development of broadband.

Evaluating Funding Opportunities

This section highlights communities that have received funding from federal programs, including the Affordable Connectivity Program (ACP) and the Emergency Broadband Benefit (EBB), which have primarily supported both tribal and non-tribal communities in improving broadband access. Community Anchor Institutions—such as schools, libraries, and healthcare facilities—are also mapped, as they serve as key criteria in determining eligibility for federal funding. Mapping these locations provides a clearer understanding of where funding has been allocated, reveals gaps in coverage, and helps identify areas where additional investment is needed to support underserved communities.

Federally Funded, Tribally Owned Broadband Case Studies

This map examines California's current middle-mile broadband infrastructure alongside the state’s recommended and planned buildout goals. It provides insights into the state’s existing broadband capabilities, including areas of strength and significant gaps in infrastructure. This analysis serves as a foundation for understanding California’s potential to connect underserved communities, focusing on the critical middle-mile infrastructure required to support last-mile connectivity in rural and Indigenous areas.
