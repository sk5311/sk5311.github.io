Segregation, Desegregation, and Resegregation in Washington DC

>

Washington D.C. has a long and complex history shaped by segregation and exclusionary policies that have left an indelible mark on its neighborhoods and communities. From the institutionalized practices of redlining to discriminatory federal housing policies, these historical forces have entrenched patterns of racial and economic inequality. While such practices are no longer as overt, their effects continue to manifest in subtler forms, perpetuating displacement and gentrification. These legacies are evident in the city’s spatial patterns, where rising property values and redevelopment projects often lead to the dislocation of marginalized communities. This analysis aims to visualize the remnants of inequitable policies and explores Navy Yard as a case study of how subtle, yet intentional, forces of exclusion and displacement persist in reshaping the District.

The Federal Housing Administration Grading Map (1937)

The FHA was established in 1934 to subsidize private development of new housing development and encourage homeownership by unsuring mortgage loans. The grading map that they produced essentailly institutionalized race as a criterion for loan approvals. Prologue DC found that since the Administration's inception to 1964, 98% of loans that were approved were given to white residents. The georeferenced map is not color coded, but Grades A-E were given graduated shades of green and were associated with white communities. The grades starting from G are represented in shades of red, and represented the lowest grade of residential property, which helped white residents know which neighborhoods to avoid. Although out-right discriminatory tools, such as this FHA Grading Map is now illegal, the spatial patterns that exist today still reflect the impacts of these discriminatory policies. Case Study: Navy Yard

Navy Yard, a neighborhood located in Southeast Washington DC and sits north of the Anacostia River. This Riverfront neighborhood was once home to the Washington Navy Yard, and was an important employer for African American Communities. With the implementation of the Navy Yard Metro Station and the National Parks baseball stadium, many existing residents were "compensated" to leave and many of the historical dwellings were demolished to make room for new units and residents. This section of this analysis will explore a localized example of how Black communities in the District were displaced with an influx of white residents. The Anacostia River, and the location that it is situated, plays a unique symbolic role in highlighting the disparities that are visible from above.
