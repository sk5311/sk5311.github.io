Segregation, Desegregation, and Resegregation in Washington DC

>

Washington D.C. has a long and complex history shaped by segregation and exclusionary policies that have left an indelible mark on its neighborhoods and communities. From the institutionalized practices of redlining to discriminatory federal housing policies, these historical forces have entrenched patterns of racial and economic inequality. While such practices are no longer as overt, their effects continue to manifest in subtler forms, perpetuating displacement and gentrification. These legacies are evident in the city’s spatial patterns, where rising property values and redevelopment projects often lead to the dislocation of marginalized communities. This analysis aims to visualize the remnants of inequitable policies and explores Navy Yard as a case study of how subtle, yet intentional, forces of exclusion and displacement persist in reshaping the District.

The Federal Housing Administration Grading Map (1937)

The FHA was established in 1934 to subsidize private development of new housing development and encourage homeownership by unsuring mortgage loans. The grading map that they produced essentailly institutionalized race as a criterion for loan approvals. Prologue DC found that since the Administration's inception to 1964, 98% of loans that were approved were given to white residents. The georeferenced map is not color coded, but Grades A-E were given graduated shades of green and were associated with white communities. The grades starting from G are represented in shades of red, and represented the lowest grade of residential property, which helped white residents know which neighborhoods to avoid. Although out-right discriminatory tools, such as this FHA Grading Map is now illegal, the spatial patterns that exist today still reflect the impacts of these discriminatory policies.
